\begin{abstract}
mmWave radar enables all-weather, non-contact human sensing, yet its inherent
sparsity of around 100 reflections per frame severely limits downstream body analysis.
Existing point cloud completion methods target dense rigid-object inputs and
fail under extreme sparsity combined with non-rigid human body motion across actions.
To address this, we present PRISM, a conditional latent diffusion framework
for reconstructing human point clouds from sparse mmWave radar.
A skeleton keypoint detector extracts and aligns skeletons from sparse radar
to the radar coordinate frame; a geometric auto-encoder extracts stable coarse
geometry from the raw mmWave cloud.
Together with skeleton topology from a graph convolutional network and action
class embeddings, these form a 145-token conditioning sequence that guides a
Dynamic Transformer to denoise the latent representation under a VP-SDE framework,
generating LiDAR-quality human point clouds.
To evaluate performance under sparse conditions, we introduce MIST, the first
mmWave dataset providing simultaneously paired clear-view and through-obstacle captures.
On MIST under clear-view conditions, PRISM generates temporally coherent point
cloud sequences guided by per-frame skeleton constraints, and achieves a COV-CD
of 0.362 against 0.113 for the strongest completion baseline.
Under through-obstacle attenuation, where radar returns decrease to around
50 points per frame and all completion baselines produce fixed-template outputs,
PRISM maintains meaningful reconstruction.
Code and dataset are available at \url{https://mmwave-human.github.io/PRISM/}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

% ── Teaser figure ───────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering

  \caption{
    Four frames of a bowing sequence.
    Completion and mmWave baselines produce near-identical standing shapes regardless of pose,
    while PRISM faithfully tracks the full bow arc guided by per-frame skeleton conditioning.
  }
  \label{fig:teaser}
\end{figure}

Millimeter-wave FMCW radar has emerged as an increasingly attractive modality
for non-intrusive human sensing.
Unlike optical sensors, it penetrates non-metallic obstacles and operates
reliably under complete darkness and adverse weather~\cite{yang2023mmfi,mmwavesurvey}.
These characteristics have enabled its adoption across a broad range of
body-centric tasks, including human activity recognition~\cite{yang2023mmfi,radhar,har_sensors2025},
skeletal pose estimation~\cite{mmhpe,radarskelet,virteach,points},
and multi-person localization and tracking~\cite{m3pose}.
Since the sensor produces only sparse geometric point clouds rather than
photorealistic imagery, it carries no visual record of the subject's appearance,
making it particularly well suited for healthcare monitoring and other
privacy-sensitive scenarios where camera-based sensing is impractical or
socially unacceptable.

Due to physical antenna aperture and bandwidth constraints, a human body in a
typical indoor environment produces only around 150 radar reflections per frame,
far fewer than the thousands of measurements generated by a LiDAR scanner.
Partial occlusion by furniture or walls further attenuates returns, leaving the
resulting point cloud highly incomplete with missing body regions that fluctuate
unpredictably across frames.
Neither general-purpose point cloud completion methods~\cite{pcn,pointr,snowflake,lakenet}
nor mmWave-specific reconstruction approaches~\cite{mmpoint,mmbody} can associate
sparse radar returns with specific anatomical regions or produce pose-specific outputs.
Completion methods are designed for dense partial observations of rigid synthetic
objects, and their nearest-neighbor feature aggregation degrades severely under
extreme sparsity, producing semantically empty features for body keypoints in
occluded or sparsely sampled regions.
Without modeling the sequential evolution of body geometry across an action,
mmWave reconstruction methods produce a fixed average-body output regardless of
the action performed, leaving action categories indistinguishable across frames,
as Figure~\ref{fig:teaser} illustrates.
Latent diffusion models~\cite{lion,tiger} offer greater distributional diversity
but carry no joint-level body structure, and conditioning them on camera-derived
inputs to supply body priors sacrifices the privacy advantage that makes mmWave
radar attractive for real-world deployment.
No existing method is capable of reconstructing a complete, densely sampled human
point cloud sequence from sparse radar inputs alone.

To this end, we propose PRISM, a skeleton-guided latent diffusion framework
that reconstructs dense human point clouds from sparse mmWave radar alone.
The key insight is that the human skeleton provides precisely the structural
prior needed to constrain such an underdetermined problem.
Skeletal joint positions encode the kinematic topology of the body and serve as
a natural bridge between sparse radar observations and complete body shape.
To preserve the privacy advantages of mmWave radar, we introduce SkelKD, a
lightweight skeleton detector trained with depth-camera skeleton annotations as
ground-truth supervision, that learns to predict radar-aligned joint positions
directly from sparse mmWave point clouds.
Its joint-level structural conditioning guides the denoising process to follow
the kinematic evolution of the action sequence, producing temporally coherent
reconstructions that evolve continuously with body motion.
Skeleton conditioning alone, however, is insufficient under severe occlusion.
Missing radar returns across large body regions cause standard
farthest-point-sampling to yield semantically empty features for those areas.
We therefore introduce GeoAE, a geometric auto-encoder that maps radar inputs
of any density to a fixed set of stable coarse body points, providing consistent
geometric coverage where conventional sampling strategies collapse.
Together with per-action categorical embeddings, SkelKD and GeoAE form a
three-path conditioning signal that guides a Dynamic Transformer through every
step of VP-SDE denoising in a structured latent space, with LiDAR point clouds
as the diffusion reconstruction target, yielding temporally coherent,
action-discriminative outputs from mmWave radar alone.
To evaluate robustness under extreme sparsity, we introduce MIST, the first
mmWave dataset providing simultaneously paired clear-view and through-obstacle
captures; under through-obstacle conditions with an 18\,mm wooden board
at varying occlusion degrees, PRISM maintains a COV-CD of 0.256 while all
deterministic baselines collapse below 0.05.
Our contributions are:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em,itemsep=2pt]

  \item \textbf{SkelKD} is a lightweight skeleton detector that resolves the
        coordinate misalignment between depth-camera conventions and the radar
        frame without explicit calibration targets.
        Trained with depth-camera skeleton annotations as ground-truth supervision,
        it predicts radar-aligned joint positions directly from sparse mmWave
        point clouds via joint-query cross-attention over 17 body joints,
        eliminating the need for any camera at inference and enabling fully
        mmWave-only deployment.

  \item \textbf{GeoAE} addresses the density-aggregation failure that occurs
        when farthest-point-sampling is applied to extremely sparse mmWave inputs.
        A compact encoder-decoder maps radar clouds of any density to 128 stable
        coarse body points, providing full-body geometric coverage that remains
        consistent even when large regions are absent due to occlusion or
        through-obstacle attenuation.

  \item \textbf{Three-path condition injection.}
        SkelKD, GeoAE, and an action class embedding are assembled into a
        145-token K/V sequence that is injected at every cross-attention layer
        of the Dynamic Transformer, with action semantics further modulating
        every Adaptive LayerNorm via Classifier-Free Guidance.
        This joint skeleton--geometry--action conditioning constrains latent
        VP-SDE denoising to follow the kinematic evolution of each action,
        producing temporally coherent, action-discriminative point cloud
        sequences that no existing unconditional or single-stream diffusion
        model achieves.

  \item \textbf{MIST} fills the gap left by existing mmWave datasets, none of
        which evaluate through-obstacle deployment.
        Paired clear-view and through-obstacle captures with synchronized 3-D
        skeleton ground truth across multiple occlusion levels enable PRISM to be
        validated under the extreme sparsity regime where all baselines collapse,
        demonstrating that skeleton-guided diffusion generalizes to real-world
        obstacle conditions without domain adaptation.
        Synchronized LiDAR, depth, and mmWave streams further support multi-modal
        research, and the action set covers rehabilitation and daily-living motions
        relevant to healthcare monitoring.

\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{mmWave-based human sensing.}
mmWave FMCW radar enables non-intrusive human sensing with privacy-preserving
properties that optical sensors cannot provide~\cite{mmwavesurvey}.
Early work demonstrated that raw mmWave point cloud sequences carry sufficient
spatiotemporal cues for human activity recognition~\cite{radhar}, and subsequent
methods introduced fine-grained spatiotemporal feature extraction to improve
discriminability across similar actions~\cite{har_sensors2025}.
Skeleton pose estimation from radar emerged as the next challenge: kinematic-
constrained learning recovered 3-D joint positions from radar returns~\cite{radarskelet},
and virtual-to-real knowledge transfer addressed the persistent training-data
scarcity~\cite{virteach}.
Recent findings show that lightweight per-joint feature aggregation matches or
outperforms dense global Transformer encoders at substantially lower cost~\cite{points,mmhpe},
and geometry-aware temporal chaining further improves robustness against
intermittent frame-level miss-detections~\cite{mmchainpose}.
Multi-person scenarios have been addressed via cluster-based person
separation~\cite{m3pose}.
On the reconstruction side, mmBody~\cite{mmbody} targets parametric mesh
recovery from radar, and mmPoint~\cite{mmpoint} conditions a multi-resolution
encoder-decoder on a deformable body template for single-frame dense generation.
More recent work pushes toward richer reconstructions: mmDEAR~\cite{mmdear}
introduces a two-stage pipeline that first densifies the sparse point cloud and
then fits an SMPL body model, while mmDiffusion~\cite{mmdiffusion} is the first
method to formulate sequential dense human generation as a diffusion denoising
problem over temporal mmWave observations.
Diffusion-based signal enhancement has also been applied to automotive mmWave
radar using range-image guidance~\cite{mmwavediff}.
Despite this progress, all existing methods assume obstacle-free sensor
placement, and reconstruction approaches rely on dense multi-frame accumulation
or deterministic regression without modelling the body-structural uncertainty
that extreme per-frame sparsity introduces.

\paragraph{Point cloud completion.}
Point cloud completion aims to recover a dense complete shape from a partial
observation, a problem structurally related to sparse sensor inputs.
PCN~\cite{pcn} established the coarse-to-fine paradigm with a folding-based
decoder, and PoinTr~\cite{pointr} reformulated the task as set-to-set prediction
with geometry-aware proxy grouping, substantially improving shape coverage.
SnowflakeNet~\cite{snowflake} introduced progressive skip-Transformer upsampling
for multi-scale generation, and LAKe-Net~\cite{lakenet} added unsupervised
keypoint localization to capture object topology.
AnchorFormer~\cite{anchorformer} improved fine-grained discriminability through
dynamic anchor nodes, and AdaPoinTr~\cite{adapointr} extended proxy-based
querying with adaptive geometry-aware generation.
On the generative side, DiffComplete~\cite{diffcomplete} casts shape completion
as a conditional diffusion process with hierarchical spatial conditioning,
demonstrating that generative modelling substantially outperforms deterministic
decoders in shape diversity.
FSC~\cite{fsc} targets inputs as sparse as 64 observed points via a dual-branch
design that combines comprehensive feature learning with saliency-guided
selection.
Most recently, SuperPC~\cite{superpc} unifies completion, upsampling, denoising,
and colorization within a single diffusion model by leveraging cross-task spatial
correlations.
All of these methods are designed for dense partial scans of rigid ShapeNet
objects and carry no structural prior about the human body.
Without a skeletal body prior, feature aggregation over the sparse, structureless
reflections produced by mmWave radar yields semantically empty representations
for unobserved joints, and every completion method collapses to the same average
body template regardless of the action being performed.

\paragraph{Diffusion models for 3-D generation.}
Score-based and latent diffusion models have rapidly advanced 3-D shape generation.
LION~\cite{lion} proposed a hierarchical VAE with a latent DDPM, demonstrating
that structured latent spaces yield diverse, high-quality shapes.
DiT-3D~\cite{dit3d} showed that plain Diffusion Transformers achieve
state-of-the-art unconditional generation when applied directly to voxelized
point cloud tokens, removing the need for U-Net inductive biases.
LDT~\cite{ldt} encodes point clouds into structured latent tokens and applies a
Dynamic Transformer for denoising, enabling fine-grained per-step conditional
guidance beyond global vector injection.
TIGER~\cite{tiger} introduced time-varying denoising for more temporally coherent
shape sequences.
Conditional settings have also been explored: DiffComplete~\cite{diffcomplete}
injects partial shape features via hierarchical spatial conditioning to guide
completion-oriented diffusion, and SuperPC~\cite{superpc} unifies multiple point
cloud restoration tasks within a single three-level conditioned diffusion
framework.
These advances confirm that latent diffusion with structured conditioning
provides the expressiveness needed to model complex shape distributions.
However, all existing models condition generation on a global latent vector or
on dense spatial features extracted from the partial scan itself.
None inject joint-level skeletal topology, and none address action-consistent
geometry across sequential frames, which are critical requirements when the
conditioning signal is a stream of extremely sparse radar observations where
entire body regions may be absent in any given frame.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Overview}

Figure~\ref{fig:arch} illustrates PRISM's two-path structure.

\textbf{Path~\textcircled{1}} takes the sparse mmWave point cloud
$X\!\in\!\Rbb^{B\times N\times3}$ as its sole inference-time input.
SkelKD predicts a radar-frame-aligned skeleton
$\hat{P}\!\in\!\Rbb^{B\times17\times3}$ directly from $X$, supervised at
training time by depth-camera annotations that are unavailable at inference.
A positional encoder projects the 17 joint coordinates into embedding space,
and a GCN then produces 17 skeleton tokens that encode joint-level topology.
In parallel, GeoAE maps $X$ to a 128-point stable coarse body proxy
$C\!\in\!\Rbb^{B\times128\times3}$, providing a geometry-level representation
robust to the high per-frame density variation of mmWave returns.
An action class embedding drawn from the label catalog supplies the third
conditioning signal.
The Condition Network concatenates the GCN skeleton tokens, the coarse-point
tokens, and the action embedding into a 145-token K/V sequence that is injected
at every layer of the Dynamic Transformer.
Simultaneously, the HierVAE encoder maps $X$ to the clean latent
$Z_0\!\in\!\Rbb^{B\times8\times120}$.

\textbf{Path~\textcircled{2}} corrupts $Z_0$ to $Z_t$ via the VP-SDE forward
schedule.
A Dynamic Transformer with 6 blocks and hidden dimension 256 iteratively
denoises $Z_t$: at each block, $Z_t$ serves as the query while the 145-token
K/V sequence provides structural and semantic context via cross-attention,
and AdaLN injects the timestep embedding together with the global conditioning
signal.
The denoised estimate $\hat{Z}_0$ is decoded by the HierVAE decoder to the
final dense point cloud $X_f\!\in\!\Rbb^{B\times256\times3}$.

\subsection{SkelKD: Skeleton Keypoint Detector}
\label{sec:skelkd}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{assets/SkelKD.jpg}
  \caption{
    SkelKD architecture.
    The PCN encoder extracts per-point local features $f_\text{local}$
    and a global context vector $f_\text{global}$ from the sparse mmWave input.
    A Joint Feature Aggregator uses 17 learnable joint embeddings as
    Cross-Attention queries over $f_\text{local}$, producing joint-specific
    features $f_\text{joint}$ that remain well-defined even for occluded joints.
    The concatenated representation $[f_\text{joint}\,\|\,f_\text{global}]$
    is passed to a Residual MLP regressor with zero-initialized output layer,
    yielding refined joint positions $\hat{P}_\text{joints}$ via a
    near-identity mapping at initialization.
    The depth-camera skeleton serves only as training supervision and is
    absent at inference.
  }
  \label{fig:skelkd}
\end{figure}

The sensor modalities in MIST carry independent coordinate
conventions.
LiDAR frames are mean-centered and normalized to the unit sphere using
parameters $(\bar{P}_L, s_L)$ retained for downstream alignment;
depth-camera skeletons are axis-permuted from $(\text{lateral},\text{height},
\text{depth})$ to the LiDAR convention and rescaled accordingly;
mmWave clouds are independently normalized after filtering returns beyond
20\,m, leaving a residual $\sim$3\,m systematic offset from the LiDAR frame.

SkelKD (Skeleton Keypoint Detector) takes the sparse mmWave cloud $X$ and
the raw depth-camera skeleton $P_\text{raw}$ as input, and regresses a
radar-space-aligned skeleton $\hat{P}\!\in\!\Rbb^{B\times17\times3}$ that
absorbs the residual coordinate-frame bias and occlusion-induced joint drift
without requiring any explicit calibration target.
The corrected $\hat{P}$ is then encoded by a lightweight GCN to produce
$17$ joint condition tokens that later guide the Dynamic Transformer.

\paragraph{Point cloud feature extraction and joint aggregation.}
Applying nearest-neighbor joint-to-point mapping directly to a sparse mmWave
cloud ($N\!\lesssim\!128$) is brittle: joints occluded by body pose or material
attenuation may have no nearby radar return, causing the mapping to collapse to
an arbitrary or distant point.
SkelKD addresses this through a two-stage feature extraction strategy.
First, two 1-D convolution layers ($3\!\to\!64\!\to\!128$, BatchNorm, ReLU)
process the raw point cloud to produce per-point local features
$f_\text{local}\!\in\!\Rbb^{B\times N\times128}$ capturing fine-grained
geometry; symmetric max-pooling followed by a 2-layer MLP ($128\!\to\!256$)
summarizes the entire cloud into a global context vector
$f_\text{global}\!\in\!\Rbb^{B\times256}$ that encodes overall body silhouette.
Second, a learnable $17\!\times\!128$ joint embedding table serves as
Cross-Attention queries $Q$, with $f_\text{local}$ providing keys $K$ and
values $V$:
\begin{equation}
  f_\text{joint} = \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\tfrac{QK^\top}{\sqrt{d}}\right)V,
  \quad Q\!\in\!\Rbb^{17\times128},\ K,V\!\in\!\Rbb^{N\times128}.
\end{equation}
Each query independently attends to all $N$ points, so when a joint is
occluded and no point lies nearby, the attention distribution spreads smoothly
over the visible surface rather than degenerating to a single noisy match.
The global context $f_\text{global}$ is broadcast and concatenated with
$f_\text{joint}$ to further stabilize estimation for heavily occluded joints,
yielding the final per-joint representation
$[f_\text{joint}\,\|\,f_\text{global}]\!\in\!\Rbb^{B\times17\times384}$.

\paragraph{Skeleton refinement and training.}
A shared-weight MLP ($384\!\to\!256\!\to\!128\!\to\!3$) operating on each joint
independently predicts a residual correction, which is added back to the raw
depth-camera skeleton:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{P} = P_\text{raw}
            + \mathrm{MLP}_\text{res}\!\left([f_\text{joint}\,\|\,f_\text{global}]\right),
  \quad \hat{P}\!\in\!\Rbb^{B\times17\times3}.
\end{equation}
The output layer of $\mathrm{MLP}_\text{res}$ is zero-initialized, so the
network starts as a near-identity mapping and only gradually learns to correct
systematic offsets; this prevents large early-training gradients that would
otherwise distort the depth-camera prior.
SkelKD is trained end-to-end with a composite loss
$\mathcal{L}_\text{SkelKD} = \mathcal{L}_\text{MPJPE} + 0.1\,\mathcal{L}_\text{bone}$,
where $\mathcal{L}_\text{MPJPE}$ minimizes per-joint Euclidean error and
$\mathcal{L}_\text{bone}$ penalizes length inconsistency in adjacent bone
segments to prevent anatomically implausible skeletons even when individual
joint errors are small.
Training applies Gaussian noise augmentation ($\sigma\!=\!0.03$) to the input
cloud and randomly zeros up to 4 joints (30\% probability) to simulate
radar occlusion patterns, substantially improving robustness at deployment.
SkelKD weights are frozen after pre-training so that the subsequent latent
diffusion stage trains against a fixed, stable skeleton representation.

\subsection{Three-Path Condition Network}
\label{sec:condnet}

The Condition Network assembles three complementary streams into a single
145-token K/V sequence injected at every denoising layer of the Dynamic
Transformer (Fig.~\ref{fig:arch}).

\paragraph{Stream \textcircled{1}: Skeleton GCN encoder.}
The radar-aligned skeleton $\hat{P}$ output by SkelKD is encoded by a 2-layer
GCN defined on the H36M-17 joint topology.
The symmetrically normalized adjacency matrix
$\hat{A} = D^{-1/2}(A+I)D^{-1/2}$ propagates kinematic-chain relationships
across the skeleton graph:
$H^{(l+1)} = \sigma\!\left(\hat{A}\,H^{(l)}\,W^{(l)}\right)$,
with GELU activation and LayerNorm at each layer.
This graph-structured encoding is essential for distal joints such as wrists
and ankles, where per-joint features alone provide insufficient context without
propagation through the kinematic chain.
The GCN maps each joint from $\Rbb^3$ to a 256-dimensional feature space,
producing 17 topology-aware node features $F_s$ projected to
$\mathrm{KV}_s\!\in\!\Rbb^{B\times256\times17}$;
their mean pool $g_s\!=\!\mathrm{MeanPool}(F_s)$ feeds the global modulation
signal $c$.

\paragraph{Stream \textcircled{2}: Coarse geometry via GeoAE.}
Direct application of farthest-point sampling to a sparse mmWave cloud
($N\!\sim\!50$) produces severely overlapping and semantically empty seed
points for occluded body regions.
GeoAE (Geometric Auto-Encoder) addresses this by learning a density-invariant
mapping from $X$ to 128 canonical coarse body points
$C\!\in\!\Rbb^{B\times128\times3}$.
The encoder extracts per-point features through two successive 1-D convolution
layers with progressively expanding channels, followed by symmetric max-pooling
to obtain a global feature $f_g\!\in\!\Rbb^{B\times256}$.
The decoder unfolds $f_g$ via transposed convolution into 128 seed points,
which are then refined through three MLP-Residual blocks and a final projection
layer to produce the output coordinates $C$.
GeoAE is trained jointly with the diffusion stage under
$\mathcal{L}_\text{AE} = 0.1\,\mathcal{L}_\text{CD}(C, X_\text{GT})$.
The resulting coarse points $C$ are further encoded by two point-wise
convolution layers into $\mathrm{KV}_c\!\in\!\Rbb^{B\times256\times128}$,
providing full-body geometric coverage that remains consistent even when large
regions are absent due to occlusion or through-obstacle attenuation.

\paragraph{Stream \textcircled{3}: Action class label.}
While the skeleton and coarse geometry streams provide spatial structural
information, they carry no explicit semantic label distinguishing, for example,
a rehabilitation exercise from a daily-living motion that may share similar
joint configurations at individual frames.
To resolve this ambiguity, the action class label $y\!\in\!\{0,\ldots,29\}$
is mapped to a dense embedding $e_y\!=\!\mathrm{LabelEmb}(y)$ and incorporated
into the global AdaLN modulation signal:
\begin{equation}
  c = e_t + e_y + \mathrm{Linear}(g_s),
\end{equation}
where $e_t$ is the diffusion timestep embedding and $g_s$ is the skeleton
global pool.
This signal $c$ modulates the scale and shift parameters of every Adaptive
LayerNorm in the Dynamic Transformer, ensuring that action identity
persistently biases generation throughout the entire denoising trajectory
rather than only at the input stage.
During training, each of the three streams is independently dropped with 10\%
probability to enable Classifier-Free Guidance (CFG) at inference.

The skeleton and coarse-geometry token sets are concatenated:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{KV}
    = \mathrm{cat}[\mathrm{KV}_s,\,\mathrm{KV}_c]
    \;\in\;\Rbb^{B\times256\times145},
\end{equation}
forming the 145-token K/V sequence injected into every Cross-Attention layer.

\subsection{Latent Diffusion and Dynamic Transformer}
\label{sec:diffusion}

\paragraph{Structured latent space.}
Running VP-SDE diffusion directly in the raw point cloud space is
computationally prohibitive and statistically unstable for variable-density
inputs: the number of points per mmWave frame varies unpredictably, and
point-wise noise injection does not respect the underlying surface manifold.
Following the hierarchical VAE paradigm of LION~\cite{lion}, we instead operate
diffusion in a compact, fixed-size latent space that decouples generation from
input density.
A hierarchical VAE encoder maps the sparse input $X$ into a structured latent
$Z_0\!\in\!\Rbb^{B\times8\times120}$ through six hierarchical levels, each
applying a $k\!=\!32$ radius-based local grouper, a ResidualBlock with
multi-head self-attention ($d\!=\!128$, 4~heads), and MLP expansion.
Each level contributes $z_\text{dim}\!=\!20$ VAE posterior dimensions,
and concatenating across all six levels along the feature axis yields the
$8\!\times\!120$ latent tokens ($6\!\times\!20\!=\!120$); ActNorm layers are
applied at each level to ensure numerically stable training.
The decoder initializes a learnable seed set and iteratively refines it through
posterior DecoderBlocks conditioned on each latent level; the output count
$K\!=\!256$ is decoupled from the encoder and can be adjusted at inference
without retraining.
The VAE is pre-trained with
$\mathcal{L}_\text{VAE}=\mathcal{L}_\text{CD}+\mathcal{L}_\text{EMD}
+10^{-4}\mathcal{L}_\text{KL}$ and its weights are frozen before the
diffusion stage begins, so the diffusion model learns to denoise within
a stable, pre-defined latent geometry rather than a co-evolving one.

\paragraph{VP-SDE forward process.}
A variance-preserving SDE progressively corrupts the clean latent $Z_0$ over
$T\!=\!1000$ discrete steps:
\begin{equation}
  q(Z_t\mid Z_{t-1})
    = \mathcal{N}\!\left(Z_t;\,\sqrt{1-\beta_t}\,Z_{t-1},\,\beta_t I\right),
\end{equation}
with a linear noise schedule $\beta_1\!=\!0.1$ to $\beta_T\!=\!20$.
Because the VAE latent space is compact and structured, the marginal
$q(Z_t\mid Z_0)=\mathcal{N}(Z_t;\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}Z_0,(1-\bar\alpha_t)I)$
converges cleanly to an isotropic Gaussian, providing a well-conditioned
diffusion target.
Diffusion operates entirely in the $8\!\times\!120$ latent space, keeping
the reverse process independent of the final output resolution.

\paragraph{Dynamic Transformer denoising network.}
The denoising network receives the noisy latent $Z_t\!\in\!\Rbb^{B\times8\times120}$
and predicts the score $\nabla_{Z_t}\log q(Z_t)$.
An input projection maps $Z_t$ from $z_\text{dim}\!=\!120$ to the working
dimension $d_\text{model}\!=\!256$ via a point-wise convolution.
The projected tokens are then processed by 6 sequential ResidualBlocks.
Each block first performs cross-attention between the projected latent tokens
as queries and the 145-token K/V sequence from the Condition Network as keys
and values, allowing every latent token to attend over the full structural and
semantic context provided by the skeleton, coarse geometry, and action streams.
To balance conditioning frequency and computational cost, the 145-token K/V
sequence is injected at alternate blocks, so structure-aware attention is
applied at blocks 1, 3, and 5 while blocks 2, 4, and 6 apply self-attention
only, enabling the network to consolidate local latent structure between
conditioning steps.
Following each attention operation, the global modulation signal
$c = e_t + e_y + \mathrm{Linear}(g_s)$ drives the scale and shift parameters
of Adaptive LayerNorm, ensuring that the timestep, action identity, and
skeleton-level pose jointly bias every normalization in the network.
Following the dynamic positional encoding scheme of LDT~\cite{ldt},
$e_t$ is produced by a two-layer MLP applied to sinusoidal Fourier features
of $t$; each Transformer block uses an independent linear projection of this
embedding as its AdaLN scale and shift, so the denoising trajectory is encoded
as a sequence of block-specific time modulations rather than a single shared
scalar, giving the network fine-grained awareness of both \emph{where} in the
reverse process a sample lies and \emph{which} layer is currently transforming
it.
A position-wise feed-forward sub-layer with residual connection follows each
attention block.
The output tokens are projected back to $z_\text{dim}\!=\!120$ by a final
AdaLN-conditioned linear layer, yielding the predicted noise $\hat{\epsilon}$.
At inference, the ancestral sampler runs for 200 steps with classifier-free
guidance: the denoiser is evaluated twice per step—once with the full
three-path condition sequence $\tau(Y)$ and once with all conditions
dropped—and the noise predictions are combined as
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\epsilon}
    = \epsilon_\theta(Z_t,t)
      + w\bigl(\epsilon_\theta(Z_t,t,\tau(Y)) - \epsilon_\theta(Z_t,t)\bigr),
\end{equation}
where $w$ is the guidance scale.
The resulting clean latent estimate is decoded by the frozen VAE decoder into
the final dense point cloud $X_f\!\in\!\Rbb^{B\times256\times3}$.
The training objective combines an L2 score-matching loss with an auxiliary
coarse Chamfer loss on the GeoAE output:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_\text{diff}
    = \mathbb{E}_{t,Z_0,\epsilon}\!\left[\|\epsilon - \epsilon_\theta(Z_t,t,\mathrm{KV},c)\|^2\right]
    + 0.1\,\mathcal{L}_\text{CD}(C, X_\text{GT}),
\end{equation}
where the auxiliary term ensures that the coarse geometry stream remains
geometrically grounded throughout joint training.

\section{Dataset}
\label{sec:dataset}

\subsection{MIST: mmWave Impaired-Sight Through-obstacle Dataset}
\label{sec:mist}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.75\linewidth]{assets/MIST.jpg}
  \caption{
    MIST collection setup.
    An 18\,mm wooden board is positioned at varying occlusion degrees between
    the subject and the door-side mmWave radar.
    The overhead unit provides synchronized LiDAR point cloud and 3-D skeleton ground truth.
  }
  \label{fig:mist}
\end{figure}

All existing public mmWave datasets are collected in obstacle-free environments,
leaving a critical evaluation gap for through-obstacle deployment.
MIST addresses this with a three-sensor design (Figure~\ref{fig:mist}):
an overhead unit combining a LiDAR and an Intel RealSense D435i depth camera
provides dense point cloud and synchronized 3-D skeleton ground truth
(17 joints, H36M convention);
a door-side unit combines a TI IWR6843 mmWave radar with a second RealSense D435i,
capturing attenuated radar returns through an 18\,mm wooden board whose
position relative to the subject defines the four occlusion levels.
All sensors are extrinsic-calibrated to a shared world coordinate.

\paragraph{Scale and composition.}
MIST covers 10 subjects, 6 male and 4 female aged between 20 and 35,
performing 30 action classes at 4 occlusion levels with 3 repetitions per
action-subject-level combination, yielding approximately 945k paired
clear-view and through-obstacle frames at around 297 frames per sequence.
The 30 classes span 14 rehabilitation exercises and 16 daily-living activities;
19 classes overlap with existing public benchmarks and 11 are unique to MIST,
covering rehabilitation motion and safety-critical falls absent from prior datasets.
The complete action list and sensor specifications are provided in
Appendix~\ref{app:mist}.

\paragraph{Occlusion levels.}
L0 provides clear-view captures at 50 to 150 points per frame and serves as the
primary training and clean evaluation split under conditions comparable to
existing lab-setting datasets.
L1 introduces light occlusion by positioning the board at a distance that
attenuates peripheral body returns, yielding 60 to 100 points per frame.
L2 partially occludes the torso region, further reducing density to 30 to 60
points per frame.
L3 represents full occlusion with the board placed immediately between the sensor
and subject, leaving only 15 to 30 points per frame.
The five-fold density reduction from L0 to L3 causes all FPS-based aggregation
methods and completion baselines to produce degenerate outputs, establishing
MIST L1 through L3 as a direct stress test for the density invariance that
motivates the GeoAE design.

To our knowledge, MIST is the first dataset providing simultaneously paired
clear-view and through-obstacle mmWave point clouds with synchronized 3-D
skeleton ground truth across four occlusion levels.

\subsection{MM-Fi}
\label{sec:mmfi}

MM-Fi~\cite{yang2023mmfi} is a large-scale public benchmark for mmWave-based
human sensing, comprising 40 subjects performing 27 action classes across
5 environments, with synchronized mmWave point clouds, WiFi CSI, and depth-camera
skeleton ground truth (17 joints, H36M convention).
Each mmWave frame contains 50 to 150 points under clear indoor conditions,
making it representative of typical lab-setting radar deployments.
Although MM-Fi provides no through-obstacle captures and does not pair
clear-view and occluded streams, its scale and diversity make it a widely used
reference for cross-dataset generalization.
To validate that PRISM's skeleton-guided design transfers beyond MIST, we
re-train all baselines and PRISM on MM-Fi following the same protocol as
Section~\ref{sec:impl} and evaluate on a held-out subject split.
Full results are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:mmfi_results}.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:exp}

\subsection{Implementation Details}
\label{sec:impl}

\paragraph{Architecture.}
SkelKD extracts per-point local features of dimension 128 and a global pooled
feature of dimension 256 through two convolutional layers; 17 learnable joint
embeddings of dimension 128 serve as cross-attention queries.
The hierarchical VAE encodes across 6 levels each contributing 20 latent
dimensions, yielding $8\!\times\!120$ latent tokens with hidden dimension 128,
4 attention heads, and ActNorm at each level.
GeoAE uses a feature dimension of 256 and produces 128 stable coarse body points.
The Condition Network encodes skeleton topology through a 2-layer GCN on the
H36M-17 graph with 256-dimensional node features and coarse geometry through
a Conv1d encoder of 256 channels, assembling a total of 145 K/V tokens.
The Dynamic Transformer consists of 6 residual blocks with hidden dimension 256,
4 attention heads, and Adaptive LayerNorm conditioning.

\paragraph{Training.}
All experiments run on a single NVIDIA RTX 3090 with 24\,GB memory.
Training uses MIST-L0 subjects S01--S07 across 30 actions and 3 repetitions
per subject; subjects S08--S10 serve as the validation split; all four
occlusion levels are used for evaluation with no obstacle-specific adaptation.
Training proceeds in four stages: SkelKD is pre-trained with MPJPE and
bone-length supervision and then frozen; the hierarchical VAE is trained
independently and frozen; the Dynamic Transformer, Condition Network, and
GeoAE are jointly optimized with frozen VAE weights; finally, all components
except SkelKD are fine-tuned end-to-end.
Stage~1 uses Adam with learning rate $10^{-3}$ and cosine decay over 5000
epochs at batch size 16.
Stage~2 uses Adam with learning rate $5\!\times\!10^{-5}$ over 2000 epochs
at batch size 8.
Stages~3 and 4 share Adam with learning rate $10^{-4}$, EMA decay 0.9999,
and batch size 16 over 5000 epochs.
All stages apply 500 warm-up iterations and gradient clipping at 1.0.

All deterministic baselines, including completion methods and mmPoint, exhibit
mode collapse: COV-CD stays below 0.11 and 1-NN-CD above 0.98, indicating

The bottleneck is not architectural capacity but the absence of a structural body
prior.
SnowflakeNet's progressive multi-scale upsampling reaches COV-CD of only 0.079,
worse than the simpler PCN at 0.083, because propagating a semantically empty
global feature at each scale compounds the collapse rather than correcting it.

regressing toward a mean body shape, sacrificing distributional diversity.
mmPoint, despite being designed for mmWave inputs, collapses similarly at
COV-CD of 0.097, confirming that radar-specific feature engineering does not
substitute for a generative body-structure prior.

Generative baselines LION and TIGER produce substantially higher coverage at
COV-CD of 0.185 and 0.209 by modeling the full output distribution rather than
regressing a single estimate.
Without skeleton guidance, however, they cannot resolve ambiguous poses from
sparse observations, and TIGER's COV-CD of 0.209 remains 42.7\% below PRISM.

PRISM achieves MMD-CD of 0.00491, which is 40.6\% below PoinTr and 54.2\% below
SnowflakeNet.
COV-CD reaches 0.3624, 220\% above PoinTr and 73\% above TIGER.
The 1-NN-CD of 0.9704 is substantially closer to the ideal 0.50 than all baselines.
PRISM's COV-CD of 0.3624 is 359\% above SnowflakeNet at 0.0791, showing that performance degrades
precisely in proportion to how much an architecture relies on global feature
aggregation without a structural prior.
The higher per-sample CD of 0.018 is expected for a generative model that
prioritizes distributional fidelity over point-wise regression.

and degrades COV-CD from 0.375 to 0.289, the largest single-module drop in the

skeleton conditioning.

Removing the skeleton GCN degrades distal joints such as wrists and ankles most
severely, where kinematic-chain propagation is essential, with COV-CD falling
14.2\% and MMD-CD rising 11.8\%.
Replacing it with a Flatten-MLP that discards topology further degrades COV-CD
by 17.6\% relative to the full model.
Removing GeoAE loses the geometric anchor entirely, degrading COV-CD by 20.3\%
and MMD-CD by 18.4\%.
Replacing it with FPS on raw mmWave yields the worst single-module result at
COV-CD $-26.8\%$, because at $N\!\sim\!50$ FPS produces heavily overlapping
and semantically empty seed points for occluded regions.
Removing the action class label has the smallest effect at COV-CD $-6.3\%$,
but remains meaningful for semantically similar actions that share local joint
configurations at individual frames.

Removing all three conditioning streams reduces PRISM to an unconditional
diffusion model with COV-CD of 0.183, comparable to LION at 0.185 in
Table~\ref{tab:main}, confirming that the performance gap over unconditional
generative baselines is driven entirely by the conditioning stack, not the
diffusion backbone.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented PRISM, the first skeleton-guided diffusion framework for
human point cloud completion from extremely sparse mmWave radar (50--150 pts/frame).
Three key design choices drive performance.
SkelKD eliminates depth-camera skeleton misalignment via cross-modal attention.
GeoAE converts raw mmWave into stable 128-point coarse geometry, robust even
under heavy occlusion.
A 145-token three-path K/V sequence injects joint topology, coarse geometry,
and action semantics into every denoising layer of the Dynamic Transformer.
Experiments on MIST demonstrate significant gains over completion-based and
generative baselines under both clear-view and through-obstacle conditions,
with temporally consistent action-specific generation across 30 action classes
and 4 occlusion levels.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
SkelKD relies on a depth-camera skeleton at inference; a fully radar-only
pipeline remains future work.
MIST currently covers a single barrier material (wood); generalization to
metal, concrete, or multi-wall scenarios requires further study.
End-to-end integration with downstream HAR/HPE is unexplored.

\section{Temporal Action Completion}
\label{app:temporal}

Figure~\ref{fig:temporal} shows a 10-frame sequence of action A17 Wave-Hand
reconstructed frame-by-frame by PRISM.
Each frame is generated independently using only the per-frame SkelKD skeleton
as a conditioning signal; no temporal smoothing or sequence model is applied.
Despite this per-frame independence, the reconstructed sequence traces a smooth
arm-raise arc from the resting position through peak extension and back,
consistent with the LiDAR ground truth trajectory.
Unconditional diffusion baselines and completion methods produce the same
standing body shape across all frames with no visible arm motion.
This result confirms that per-frame skeleton conditioning alone is sufficient
for temporal coherence: because SkelKD continuously tracks the joint trajectory
across frames, the three-path condition sequence carries enough structural
information to enforce consistent pose evolution without explicit temporal
modelling.

    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}

Table~\ref{tab:mist} evaluates robustness on MIST L1--L3, where attenuation
reduces mmWave returns to 15--30 points per frame.
Deterministic baselines suffer catastrophic distributional collapse, with
COV-CD dropping 57--65\% relative to MIST-L0 and 1-NN-CD rising above 0.996,
the near-degenerate extreme of a fixed-template output.
The worst degradation falls on SnowflakeNet at $-64.6\%$, consistent with its
multi-scale architecture amplifying the same degenerate global feature at each
upsampling resolution.
PRISM degrades by only 29.4\% on COV-CD, while PoinTr's COV-CD is 420\% lower at extreme density.
This asymmetric resilience directly validates GeoAE, which maps inputs of any
density to 128 stable coarse body points and provides geometric anchoring
exactly when FPS-based aggregation collapses.
As PRISM is trained exclusively on MIST-L0 and evaluated zero-shot on L1--L3,
the result confirms that GeoAE's density invariance generalizes to the obstacle
regime without any domain adaptation.

\section{Reproducibility Details}
\label{app:repro}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Dataset:} MM-Fi is publicly available~\cite{yang2023mmfi}.
        MIST will be released upon acceptance (10 subjects, 30 actions, 4
        occlusion levels, $\sim$945k frames).
  \item \textbf{Training split:} MIST-L0 subjects S01--S07 (train),
        S08--S10 (validation); test on all L0--L3 levels.
  \item \textbf{Code \& checkpoints:} Full training/evaluation code and best
        checkpoints will be released at \todo{anonymous URL}.
  \item \textbf{Random seed:} 2023 for all experiments.
  \item \textbf{Hardware:} Single NVIDIA RTX 3090 (24\,GB).\\
        Wall-clock training times:
        Stage~1 (SkelKD, 5000 ep): $\sim$13\,h;\;
        Stage~2 (HierVAE, 2000 ep): $\sim$46\,h;\;
        Stage~3 (LDT, 5000 ep): $\sim$4\,h.
\end{itemize}
