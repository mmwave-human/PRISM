\begin{abstract}
mmWave FMCW radar is an ideal modality for human sensing, offering
all-weather, non-contact, and privacy-preserving perception, yet its
inherent sparsity severely limits downstream body analysis and no existing
dataset provides a paired quantitative benchmark for dense human point
cloud reconstruction under occlusion.
To close this gap, we present MIST, the first mmWave human point
cloud dataset providing a paired quantitative benchmark for
through-obstacle dense human point cloud reconstruction.
MIST pairs clear-view and through-obstacle captures of the same subjects
performing the same actions via a physical barrier between sensor and
subject, with synchronized LiDAR as the 3D ground truth and a three-level
occlusion design converting attenuation severity into a controlled variable,
spanning 8 subjects, 30 action classes, and 640k paired frames.
To establish a method baseline on MIST and address the failure of point
cloud completion under extreme sparsity and human motion, we propose
PRISM, a skeleton-guided conditional latent diffusion framework
reconstructing dense human point clouds from sparse mmWave radar alone.
Three conditioning streams of skeleton joint positions, coarse body geometry,
and action class embeddings guide a Dynamic Transformer to denoise under
a VP-SDE framework, generating LiDAR-quality human point clouds.
On MIST, PRISM achieves a COV-CD of 0.362 against 0.113 for the strongest
completion baseline; under through-obstacle attenuation at around 50 points
per frame where all baselines collapse to fixed-template outputs,
PRISM maintains meaningful reconstruction.
Code and dataset are available at \url{https://mmwave-human.github.io/PRISM/}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

% ── Teaser figure ───────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth, height=12cm, keepaspectratio]{assets/fig/teaser.jpg}
  \caption{
    Point cloud reconstruction across three timesteps of a bowing sequence:
    upright, mid-bow, and full bow.
  }
  \label{fig:teaser}
\end{figure}

Millimeter-wave FMCW radar has emerged as an increasingly attractive modality
for non-intrusive human sensing, operating reliably under complete darkness and
adverse weather without physical contact or line-of-sight
constraints~\cite{yang2023mmfi,mmwavesurvey}.
These properties have enabled its adoption across a broad range of body-centric
tasks, including human activity recognition~\cite{yang2023mmfi,radhar,har_sensors2025},
skeletal pose estimation~\cite{mmhpe,radarskelet,virteach,points},
and multi-person localization and tracking~\cite{m3pose}.
Its point cloud output carries no photorealistic record of the subject's
appearance, making it particularly well suited for healthcare monitoring and
other privacy-sensitive scenarios.

LiDAR and RGB-D cameras produce dense, geometrically accurate reconstructions
but cannot penetrate solid obstacles, making them unsuitable for through-wall
human monitoring.
mmWave FMCW radar uniquely penetrates non-metallic barriers, yet physical antenna
aperture and bandwidth constraints limit a human body to only 50 to 150 radar
reflections per frame, far fewer than the thousands of measurements from a LiDAR
scanner, and partial occlusion by furniture or walls further attenuates these
already sparse returns.
This combination of through-obstacle capability and inherent sparsity creates a
dual challenge that no existing dataset or method addresses.
All existing public mmWave human datasets~\cite{yang2023mmfi,mri,m4human}
provide skeleton or mesh annotations while uniformly assuming unobstructed
sensor placement; datasets that include an occlusion
scene~\cite{mmbody,rfpose} realise it by covering the sensor surface with
materials rather than placing a physical barrier between sensor and subject,
precluding any simulation of real through-wall deployment and providing no
paired clear-view reference.
On the method side, general-purpose point cloud completion
methods~\cite{pcn,pointr,snowflake} are designed for dense partial
observations of rigid synthetic objects and do not model the sequential
evolution of body geometry across an action.
As Figure~\ref{fig:teaser} shows, completion and mmWave baselines produce
near-identical standing shapes at all three timesteps of a bowing sequence,
from upright through mid-bow to full bow, regardless of pose change.
No existing method is capable of reconstructing a complete, action-coherent
human point cloud sequence from sparse radar inputs alone.

To close the dataset gap, we present MIST, the first mmWave dataset with a
physical barrier between sensor and subject, providing paired clear-view and
through-obstacle captures at three controlled occlusion levels with synchronized
LiDAR ground truth.
To achieve human body reconstruction under occlusion, we propose PRISM, a
skeleton-guided conditional latent diffusion framework that infers radar-aligned
joint positions from sparse returns at each frame and injects them as structural
conditioning signals to steer the denoising trajectory toward action-coherent
dense outputs.
As Figure~\ref{fig:teaser} shows, PRISM faithfully tracks the full bow arc
while all baselines produce a static upright shape, achieving COV-CD 0.362
against 0.113 for the strongest completion baseline, with consistent gains
across three downstream healthcare monitoring tasks spanning activity
recognition, fall detection, and rehabilitation exercise assessment.
Our contributions are as follows.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em,itemsep=2pt]

  \item \textbf{MIST} is the first mmWave FMCW human point cloud dataset with
  a physical barrier placed between sensor and subject, providing paired
  clear-view and through-obstacle captures with synchronized overhead LiDAR
  point clouds as dense reconstruction ground truth.
  A three-level occlusion design converts attenuation severity into a
  controlled experimental variable.
  MIST covers 8 subjects and 30 action classes spanning rehabilitation and
  daily-living motions, with synchronized LiDAR, depth, and mmWave streams
  supporting multi-modal research.

  \item \textbf{PRISM} is a skeleton-guided conditional latent diffusion
  framework that reconstructs dense human point clouds from sparse mmWave
  radar.
  A skeleton keypoint detector SkelKD extracts radar-aligned joint positions,
  and a geometric auto-encoder GeoAE produces stable coarse body geometry
  robust to extreme input sparsity.
  Together with action class embeddings, these conditioning signals guide a
  Dynamic Transformer denoiser under a VP-SDE framework, constraining
  reconstruction to follow the kinematic evolution of each action.

  \item \textbf{Downstream task benchmarks.}
  Using PRISM-reconstructed sequences as input, we establish benchmarks on
  human action recognition, skeletal pose estimation, and rehabilitation
  exercise quality assessment on MIST.
  These benchmarks demonstrate that high-quality reconstruction yields
  substantial and consistent gains across the full spectrum of body analysis
  required for real-world healthcare monitoring.

\end{itemize}

\section{Related work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{mmWave human datasets.}
All existing mmWave human datasets are collected under obstacle-free
conditions.
MM-Fi~\cite{yang2023mmfi} provides synchronized multi-modal streams and
M4Human~\cite{m4human} acquires high-fidelity mesh annotations via
marker-based motion capture, yet both assume no physical barrier between
sensor and subject.
mmBody~\cite{mmbody} includes an occlusion scene but realises it by
covering the sensor surface with materials rather than placing a barrier
between sensor and subject, providing no paired clear-view reference and
no simulation of real through-wall deployment.
This systematic gap leaves through-obstacle deployment, the core advantage
of mmWave over optical sensors, without any quantitative benchmark.

\paragraph{Point cloud completion and human body reconstruction.}
Completion methods such as PCN~\cite{pcn} and PoinTr~\cite{pointr} are
designed for dense partial scans of rigid synthetic objects; under the
extreme sparsity and non-rigid articulation of human radar returns their
feature aggregation collapses to semantically empty representations.
Generative models LION~\cite{lion} and LDT~\cite{ldt} operate in
hierarchical VAE~\cite{vae} latent spaces with per-step conditioning for
expressive 3-D generation, yet both condition on global latent codes or
dense spatial features extracted from the input rather than structured
anatomical priors.
mmPoint~\cite{mmpoint} recovers dense human point clouds from sparse
mmWave signals via deterministic regression but produces a fixed
average-body template under extreme sparsity, leaving action categories
indistinguishable across frames.
None of these approaches injects joint-level skeletal topology as a
structural conditioning signal, and none is designed for action-consistent
generation from streams of extremely sparse radar observations through
physical barriers.

\paragraph{Downstream body analysis.}
MiliPoint~\cite{milipoint} establishes multi-class action classification
benchmarks on sparse mmWave radar point clouds, and temporal methods such
as mmChainPose~\cite{mmchainpose} advance skeletal pose estimation by
propagating joint geometry cues across consecutive frames; both lines of
work operate under obstacle-free sensor placement and evaluate on raw
radar returns rather than reconstructed dense clouds.
For rehabilitation exercise quality assessment, KIMORE~\cite{kimore}
provides kinematic scoring benchmarks derived from RGB-D sequences of
patients performing standardised exercises, demonstrating that precise
joint trajectory estimates translate to clinically meaningful motion
quality scores.
No existing benchmark evaluates activity recognition, pose estimation, or
rehabilitation exercise quality from point clouds reconstructed under
through-obstacle sensor conditions, leaving the downstream utility of
through-wall radar sensing without direct experimental validation.

\section{MIST Dataset}
\label{sec:mist}
% ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{assets/fig/Dataset.jpg}
  \caption{
    Overview of MIST, comprising three paired occlusion levels, 30 action
    classes spanning rehabilitation and daily-living motions, and five
    synchronized sensing modalities providing complementary ground truth.
  }
  \label{fig:mist_overview}
\end{figure}

All existing public mmWave human datasets are collected under
obstacle-free conditions, leaving a critical evaluation gap for
through-obstacle deployment.
MIST addresses this by placing a physical barrier between sensor and
subject, providing the first paired quantitative benchmark for
through-obstacle dense human point cloud reconstruction.
Three occlusion levels are defined by the cumulative thickness of 18\,mm
wooden boards placed between the right-side radar and the subject:
OL0 uses no board and provides a clear line-of-sight, OL1 interposes one
18\,mm board, and OL2 stacks two boards to a total thickness of 36\,mm.
The 18\,mm board material matches the signal attenuation characteristics
of typical indoor partition materials while allowing reproducible,
incremental thickness control.
A detailed comparison with existing mmWave human datasets is provided
in Appendix~\ref{app:mist_compare}.

\paragraph{Sensor configuration.}
Two TI IWR6843 FMCW radars are co-located at door height ($\sim$1.0\,m):
one provides a clear-view path for OL0 captures and one operates through
the barrier at OL1 and OL2; collecting both streams simultaneously
guarantees that every paired frame is captured at an identical subject pose.
An overhead Livox Mid-360 LiDAR and a door-side Intel RealSense D435i
depth camera, extrinsic-calibrated to a shared world coordinate frame,
provide dense point clouds and 17-joint H36M skeleton ground truth~\cite{h36m}.
Skeleton annotations are extracted at 30\,fps and resampled to 10\,fps
to match the radar frame rate.
Sensor specifications are listed in Appendix~\ref{app:mist_sensor}.

\paragraph{Occlusion levels and statistics.}
MIST defines three occlusion levels by cumulative board thickness.
OL0, with no board in place, provides clear-view conditions with
50--150 points per frame and serves as the primary training and evaluation split.
OL1, with one 18\,mm board, attenuates returns to 25--80 points per frame,
and OL2, with two stacked boards totalling 36\,mm, further reduces returns
to 10--30 points per frame.
The approximately five-fold density reduction from OL0 to OL2 causes
FPS-based feature aggregation and all completion baselines to produce
degenerate outputs, making OL1 and OL2 a direct stress test for
density-invariant reconstruction.
MIST covers 8 subjects performing 30 action classes across 3 occlusion
levels with 3 repetitions per subject, yielding approximately 640k
paired frames.

\paragraph{Action categories.}
The 30 action classes span two functional categories.
The rehabilitation category R01 to R14 covers 14 classes targeting
upper and lower limb mobility, balance, and gait assessment relevant
to clinical motion screening.
The daily-living category D01 to D16 covers 16 classes of whole-body
movements frequent in elderly-care monitoring, including D16 Forward
Fall which directly supports fall detection as a downstream task.
Of the 30 classes, 8 share equivalent kinematic categories with MM-Fi,
enabling direct cross-dataset transfer evaluation; the complete action
list is provided in Appendix~\ref{app:mist_actions}.


\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

% ── Full architecture figure ─────────────────────────────────
\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{assets/fig/PRISM architecture overview.jpg}
  \caption{
    Overview of PRISM's two-path architecture: a condition path assembling skeleton,
    geometry, and action signals, and a diffusion path denoising a structured latent
    guided by those conditions to recover the dense point cloud.
  }
  \label{fig:arch}
\end{figure}
PRISM follows the two-path design in Figure~\ref{fig:arch}.
The condition path processes the sparse mmWave cloud $X$ through SkelKD,
GeoAE, and an action class embedding, assembling the three streams into a
condition K/V sequence that is injected at every denoising layer.
The diffusion path encodes $X$ into a compact structured latent via HierVAE,
iteratively denoises it under a VP-SDE schedule with the condition K/V driving
each Dynamic Transformer layer, and decodes the result to a dense output point cloud.

\subsection{SkelKD: skeleton keypoint detector}
\label{sec:skelkd}

SkelKD takes the sparse mmWave cloud $X$ as its sole input and regresses
radar-aligned skeleton joints $\hat{P}\!\in\!\Rbb^{B\times17\times3}$,
using depth-camera annotations only as training supervision.
Recent benchmarks on sparse mmWave point clouds show that lightweight MLP
and convolution designs consistently match or outperform transformer-based
architectures: with fewer than 200 returns per frame, self-attention is
starved of sufficient local context to be beneficial~\cite{milipoint,pointmlp}.
SkelKD therefore uses a lightweight convolution-based feature extractor~\cite{pointnet};
the predicted $\hat{P}$ is then encoded by a GCN into 17 joint condition
tokens that guide the Dynamic Transformer.

Nearest-neighbor joint-to-point mapping degenerates when joints are occluded or
attenuated, collapsing to an arbitrary distant return.
SkelKD instead uses 17 learnable joint query embeddings as cross-attention~\cite{transformer}
queries over per-point features extracted by two Conv1D layers,
$f_\text{local}\!\in\!\Rbb^{B\times N\times128}$,
concatenated with a global silhouette descriptor
$f_\text{global}\!\in\!\Rbb^{B\times256}$:
\begin{equation}
  f_\text{joint} = \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\tfrac{QK^\top}{\sqrt{d}}\right)V,
  \quad Q\!\in\!\Rbb^{17\times128},\ K,V\!\in\!\Rbb^{N\times128}.
\end{equation}
Each query attends over all $N$ points, so occluded joints spread attention
across visible returns rather than collapsing to a single noisy match.
A shared-weight MLP then maps
$[f_\text{joint}\,\|\,f_\text{global}]\!\in\!\Rbb^{B\times17\times384}$
to 3-D coordinates:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{P} = \mathrm{MLP}_\text{reg}\!\left([f_\text{joint}\,\|\,f_\text{global}]\right),
  \quad \hat{P}\!\in\!\Rbb^{B\times17\times3}.
\end{equation}
SkelKD is trained with
$\mathcal{L}_\text{SkelKD} = \mathcal{L}_\text{MPJPE} + 0.1\,\mathcal{L}_\text{bone}$,
with Gaussian noise augmentation at $\sigma\!=\!0.03$ and random joint masking
of up to 4 joints at 30\% probability to simulate radar occlusion.
Weights are frozen after pre-training so the diffusion stage trains against a
stable skeleton representation.

\subsection{Three-path condition network}
\label{sec:condnet}

The Condition Network assembles three complementary streams into a single
145-token K/V sequence injected at every denoising layer of the Dynamic
Transformer.

\paragraph{Skeleton GCN encoder.}
The radar-aligned skeleton $\hat{P}$ output by SkelKD is encoded by a 2-layer
GCN~\cite{gcn} defined on the H36M-17 joint topology~\cite{h36m}.
The symmetrically normalized adjacency matrix
$\hat{A} = D^{-1/2}(A+I)D^{-1/2}$ propagates kinematic-chain relationships
across the skeleton graph:
$H^{(l+1)} = \sigma\!\left(\hat{A}\,H^{(l)}\,W^{(l)}\right)$,
with GELU activation and LayerNorm~\cite{layernorm} at each layer.
This graph-structured encoding is essential for distal joints such as wrists
and ankles, where per-joint features alone provide insufficient context.
The GCN maps each joint to a 256-dimensional feature space,
producing 17 topology-aware node features $F_s$ projected to
$\mathrm{KV}_s\!\in\!\Rbb^{B\times256\times17}$;
their mean pool $g_s\!=\!\mathrm{MeanPool}(F_s)$ feeds the global modulation
signal $c$.

\paragraph{Coarse geometry via GeoAE.}
Direct application of farthest-point sampling to a sparse mmWave cloud
with $N\!\sim\!50$ produces severely overlapping and semantically empty seed
points for occluded body regions.
GeoAE addresses this by learning a density-invariant
mapping from $X$ to 128 canonical coarse body points
$C\!\in\!\Rbb^{B\times128\times3}$.
The encoder extracts per-point features through two successive 1-D convolution
layers, followed by symmetric max-pooling to obtain a global feature $f_g\!\in\!\Rbb^{B\times256}$.
The decoder unfolds $f_g$ via transposed convolution into 128 seed points,
which are then refined through three MLP-Residual blocks~\cite{resnet} and a final projection
layer to produce the output coordinates $C$.
GeoAE is trained jointly with the diffusion stage under
$\mathcal{L}_\text{AE} = 0.1\,\mathcal{L}_\text{CD}(C, X_\text{GT})$.
The resulting coarse points $C$ are further encoded by two point-wise
convolution layers into $\mathrm{KV}_c\!\in\!\Rbb^{B\times256\times128}$,
providing full-body geometric coverage stable under occlusion and
through-obstacle attenuation.

\paragraph{Action class label.}
The skeleton and coarse geometry streams carry no explicit semantic label
distinguishing, for example, a rehabilitation exercise from a daily-living
motion with similar joint configurations.
The action class label $y\!\in\!\{0,\ldots,29\}$
is mapped to a dense embedding $e_y\!=\!\mathrm{LabelEmb}(y)$ and incorporated
into the global AdaLN modulation signal:
\begin{equation}
  c = e_t + e_y + \mathrm{Linear}(g_s),
\end{equation}
where $e_t$ is the diffusion timestep embedding and $g_s$ is the skeleton
global pool.
This signal $c$ modulates the scale and shift parameters of every Adaptive
LayerNorm in the Dynamic Transformer, ensuring action identity biases
the full denoising trajectory.
During training, each of the three streams is independently dropped with 10\%
probability to enable Classifier-Free Guidance (CFG)~\cite{cfg} at inference.

The skeleton and coarse-geometry token sets are concatenated:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{KV}
    = \mathrm{cat}[\mathrm{KV}_s,\,\mathrm{KV}_c]
    \;\in\;\Rbb^{B\times256\times145},
\end{equation}
forming the 145-token K/V sequence injected into every Cross-Attention layer.

\subsection{Latent diffusion and dynamic transformer}
\label{sec:diffusion}

\paragraph{Structured latent space.}
Diffusing directly in raw point cloud space is statistically unstable for
variable-density inputs and computationally prohibitive.
Following~\cite{lion,ldm}, we operate in a compact, fixed-size latent space that
decouples generation from input density.
A hierarchical VAE~\cite{vae} encoder maps $X$ into a structured latent
$Z_0\!\in\!\Rbb^{B\times8\times120}$ through six levels of local grouping,
residual self-attention, and VAE posterior sampling; the decoder produces
$K\!=\!256$ output points independently of the encoder resolution.
The VAE is pre-trained with
$\mathcal{L}_\text{VAE}=\mathcal{L}_\text{CD}+\mathcal{L}_\text{EMD}
+10^{-4}\mathcal{L}_\text{KL}$~\cite{emd} and then frozen, so the denoiser trains within
a stable, pre-defined latent geometry.
Full architecture hyperparameters are in Appendix~\ref{app:arch}.

\paragraph{VP-SDE forward process.}
A variance-preserving SDE~\cite{song2021score,ddpm} corrupts the clean latent $Z_0$ over $T\!=\!1000$ steps:
\begin{equation}
  q(Z_t\mid Z_{t-1})
    = \mathcal{N}\!\left(Z_t;\,\sqrt{1-\beta_t}\,Z_{t-1},\,\beta_t I\right),
\end{equation}
with a linear schedule $\beta_1\!=\!0.1$ to $\beta_T\!=\!20$.
The compact structured latent ensures that the marginal
$q(Z_t\mid Z_0)=\mathcal{N}(Z_t;\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}Z_0,(1-\bar\alpha_t)I)$
converges to an isotropic Gaussian, giving a well-conditioned reverse process.

\paragraph{Dynamic Transformer denoising network.}
The denoising network receives $Z_t\!\in\!\Rbb^{B\times8\times120}$ and predicts
the score $\nabla_{Z_t}\log q(Z_t)$.
A point-wise projection maps $Z_t$ to $d_\text{model}\!=\!256$;
six ResidualBlocks then alternate cross-attention over the 145-token K/V sequence
(blocks 1, 3, 5) with self-attention only (blocks 2, 4, 6).
Following~\cite{ldt,dit}, each block applies an independent AdaLN modulation driven
by $c = e_t + e_y + \mathrm{Linear}(g_s)$, coupling timestep, action, and coarse
pose into every normalization layer.
The output tokens are projected back to $z_\text{dim}\!=\!120$ to yield the
predicted noise $\hat{\epsilon}$.
At inference, 200-step ancestral sampling with classifier-free guidance~\cite{cfg}
combines conditioned and unconditional predictions:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\epsilon}
    = \epsilon_\theta(Z_t,t)
      + w\bigl(\epsilon_\theta(Z_t,t,\tau(Y)) - \epsilon_\theta(Z_t,t)\bigr),
\end{equation}
where $w$ is the guidance scale; the decoded latent yields
$X_f\!\in\!\Rbb^{B\times256\times3}$.
The training objective is:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_\text{diff}
    = \mathbb{E}_{t,Z_0,\epsilon}\!\left[\|\epsilon - \epsilon_\theta(Z_t,t,\mathrm{KV},c)\|^2\right]
    + 0.1\,\mathcal{L}_\text{CD}(C, X_\text{GT}),
\end{equation}
where the auxiliary term keeps the coarse geometry stream geometrically grounded.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}
% ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.75\linewidth]{assets/fig/MIST.jpg}
  \caption{
    MIST collection setup: no board for OL0, one 18\,mm board for OL1, and
    two stacked 18\,mm boards for OL2 are placed between the subject and the

    depth camera remain unoccluded to provide ground truth.
  }
  \label{fig:mist_setup}
\end{figure}

Data collection took place in a controlled indoor laboratory with
approximately 5\,m by 6\,m usable floor area, as illustrated in
Figure~\ref{fig:mist_setup}.
Subjects performed all actions at 2 to 3\,m from the sensor array.
For OL0 no board was placed; for OL1 one 18\,mm wooden board was
interposed between the right-side mmWave radar and the subject; for OL2
two boards were stacked to a total thickness of 36\,mm to increase attenuation.
The left-side LiDAR, mmWave radar, and depth camera remained unoccluded
throughout, collecting complete unoccluded captures as dense reconstruction
ground truth and ensuring that ground truth quality is unaffected by
board placement.
All sensors were extrinsic-calibrated to a shared world coordinate frame
prior to data collection, with calibration parameters held fixed across
all subjects and sessions.

\paragraph{Training and evaluation protocol.}
Subjects P01 to P06 form the training split and P07 to P08 the held-out
evaluation split.
All three occlusion levels are evaluated without obstacle-specific
adaptation, providing a zero-shot test of density-invariant generalization.
Training follows four sequential stages: SkelKD pretraining, HierVAE
pretraining, joint optimization of the Dynamic Transformer with frozen VAE,
and end-to-end fine-tuning; all stages use Adam~\cite{adam} with cosine learning-rate
decay and full hyperparameters are listed in Appendix~\ref{app:training}.
Cross-dataset generalization results, where all models are trained on
MIST and evaluated directly on MM-Fi~\cite{yang2023mmfi} using the
8 shared action classes, are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:mmfi_results}.

\subsection{Quantitative results on MIST}

COV-CD measures the fraction of ground-truth shapes matched by at least one
generated sample, rewarding broad coverage of the target distribution.
MMD-CD measures the average distance from each ground-truth shape to its nearest
generated counterpart, rewarding distributional fidelity.
Results are reported per action and averaged over all 30 action classes.

\paragraph{Results.}
Table~\ref{tab:main} shows that all seven baselines fail to produce
action-discriminative reconstructions, though for different reasons.
The four deterministic completion backbones cluster tightly between COV-CD 0.079
and 0.113, confirming that the ceiling is set by the absence of a body prior rather
than by model capacity.
SnowflakeNet underperforms even PCN because its multi-stage farthest-point sampling
compounds density sensitivity at each upsampling level under extreme sparsity.
mmPoint achieves COV-CD 0.097, barely above the completion cluster, because
single-frame deterministic regression cannot resolve pose ambiguity from a sparse,
action-agnostic radar input.
LION and TIGER fare noticeably better by sampling from a learned distribution,
achieving COV-CD 0.185 and 0.209 respectively.
Their remaining gap relative to PRISM reveals the limit of distribution diversity
without structural grounding: the generated shapes cover the marginal body
distribution but are not steered toward any particular action.

PRISM achieves a COV-CD of 0.362 and an MMD-CD of $4.91\!\times\!10^{-3}$,
representing a $3.2\times$ improvement in coverage over PoinTr and a 40.6\%
reduction in matching distance, with consistent action discrimination across
Squat, Wave Hand, and Kick.
Qualitative cross-action comparisons and temporal reconstruction examples are
presented in Appendix~\ref{app:qual_main}.

\subsection{Downstream task evaluation}
\label{sec:downstream}

PRISM reconstructs dense human point clouds from mmWave radar alone; these
reconstructions substantially outperform raw mmWave input across all three tasks
and all occlusion levels, demonstrating that high-quality reconstruction directly
translates to downstream task gains.
We evaluate using a shared PointNet++ backbone~\cite{pointnet,qi2017pointnet2} with a
lightweight task-specific head trained independently per input condition.
We compare raw mmWave input, PoinTr as the strongest completion baseline, TIGER as
the strongest generative baseline, and PRISM across OL0 to OL2.
An unoccluded LiDAR oracle provides a modality upper bound at HAR 85.6\%, Fall F1
0.912, HPE 58\,mm, and Rehab $r\!=\!0.79$; because its input modality differs from
all compared methods, it is reported here for reference rather than listed as a table row.
Table~\ref{tab:downstream} shows that the reconstruction advantage over raw mmWave
grows as occlusion severity increases.

\paragraph{Human activity recognition (HAR).}
A two-layer MLP with layer widths 512, 256, and 30 and dropout 0.3 is trained for
30-class classification on subjects P01 to P06 and evaluated on P07 to P08.
PRISM achieves 73.1\% top-1 accuracy at OL0 versus 52.3\% for raw mmWave; under
heavy occlusion at OL2 the gap widens to 24.1 percentage points at 59.3\% versus
35.2\%.
For fall detection, PRISM reaches F1\,=\,0.851 against 0.628 for raw mmWave.

\paragraph{Human pose estimation (HPE).}
A 17-joint regression head predicts H36M-convention joint coordinates; MPJPE is
reported in millimetres.
PRISM reduces MPJPE from 138\,mm to 84\,mm at OL0, and at the most severe level OL2
still achieves 112\,mm, which remains below raw mmWave under clear-view OL0
conditions at 138\,mm, demonstrating that reconstruction compensates for substantial
signal attenuation.
The gap between PRISM and raw mmWave grows from 54\,mm at OL0 to 79\,mm at OL2.

\paragraph{Rehabilitation exercise quality assessment.}
Per-joint velocity cosine similarity is used to derive motion quality scores for R01
to R14; Pearson correlation with LiDAR reference scores is reported.
PRISM reaches $r\!=\!0.68$ at OL0 against $r\!=\!0.51$ for PoinTr and $r\!=\!0.38$
for raw mmWave, and maintains $r\!=\!0.53$ at OL2 while raw mmWave falls to
$r\!=\!0.24$, confirming that PRISM supports clinically meaningful exercise monitoring
without unobstructed sensor placement even under severe through-obstacle attenuation.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented MIST, the first through-obstacle mmWave human benchmark with three
paired occlusion levels and synchronized LiDAR ground truth, and PRISM, a
skeleton-guided latent diffusion framework that reconstructs dense human point
clouds from sparse mmWave radar alone.
PRISM resolves the two failure modes of prior approaches by inferring radar-aligned
skeleton joints from sparse returns, pairing them with density-invariant coarse
geometry and action embeddings, and injecting this structural prior at every
denoising step of a latent diffusion model.
On MIST, PRISM achieves COV-CD 0.362 against 0.113 for the strongest completion
baseline and maintains action-discriminative reconstruction at OL2 where all
deterministic baselines collapse; downstream evaluations confirm that PRISM
reconstructions consistently outperform raw mmWave at every occlusion level, with
OL2 outputs still surpassing raw mmWave under clear-view conditions.
Together, MIST and PRISM provide a foundation for privacy-preserving,
through-obstacle healthcare monitoring using mmWave radar alone.

\subsection{Comparison with Existing mmWave Datasets}
\label{app:mist_compare}

Table~\ref{tab:mist_compare} compares representative mmWave human sensing datasets along
five axes: \emph{Phys.\ Barrier}, indicating a physical object between sensor and subject;
\emph{Paired}, indicating simultaneous clear-view and occluded captures of identical poses;
\emph{LiDAR GT}, indicating synchronized overhead LiDAR as dense 3-D reconstruction ground truth;
\emph{Skel/Mesh GT}, indicating skeleton or mesh annotations; and \emph{OL}, indicating the
number of controlled occlusion levels.
RF-Pose uses sub-6\,GHz radio and produces 2-D skeleton heatmaps rather than a point cloud;
mmBody's occlusion variant covers the sensor aperture rather than placing a physical barrier
between sensor and subject.

\paragraph{Obstacle-free benchmarks.}
The dominant paradigm collects data under clear sensor--subject line-of-sight.
mmMesh~\cite{mmmesh} pioneered real-time human mesh construction from sparse 60\,GHz
reflections but used only 20 subjects across 8 actions without any occlusion scenario.
mmBody~\cite{mmbody} expanded action coverage to 100 categories and released paired mesh
annotations; its occlusion variant attenuates the signal by placing absorbent material
over the sensor aperture rather than interposing a physical barrier, yielding no
spatial selectivity, no clear-view reference, and no control over attenuation severity.
mRI~\cite{mri} introduced a multimodal benchmark that pairs mmWave with RGB-D and inertial
sensing for 3-D skeleton annotation, again under obstacle-free conditions.
MM-Fi~\cite{yang2023mmfi} uniquely includes synchronized overhead LiDAR alongside mmWave,
providing a dense reconstruction reference, but every session is collected in an open
indoor space without any barrier between sensor and subject.
M4Human~\cite{m4human} scales to 50 action categories and Vicon motion-capture mesh
annotations, providing the largest mmWave-to-mesh benchmark to date, yet retains the
same obstacle-free assumption.
Collectively, these datasets cannot evaluate degradation of reconstruction quality as
radar returns are progressively attenuated by a real physical obstacle.

\paragraph{Through-barrier and simulated-occlusion sensing.}
RF-Pose~\cite{rfpose} demonstrated that a physical wall need not block human sensing, using
sub-6\,GHz radio signals to produce 2-D skeleton heatmaps of subjects behind a wall.
However, the sub-60\,GHz frequency band, 2-D output format, single fixed wall, and absence
of a simultaneous clear-view reference or LiDAR reconstruction target leave mmWave-specific
dense point cloud reconstruction under controlled multi-level occlusion unaddressed.

\paragraph{MIST.}
MIST is the first mmWave dataset to simultaneously satisfy all three requirements.
Occlusion levels are defined by cumulative board thickness: OL0 uses no board,
OL1 interposes one 18\,mm wooden board, and OL2 stacks two boards totalling 36\,mm between
the subject and one radar; a second co-located radar maintains an unobstructed path, so
every frame provides a paired clear-view capture at OL0 and through-obstacle captures at OL1
and OL2 of the identical pose.
An overhead Livox Mid-360 LiDAR, physically separated from the barrier, provides dense
reconstruction ground truth unaffected by board placement across all levels.
The three-level occlusion design converts attenuation severity into a controlled experimental
variable, enabling the first direct evaluation of density-invariant reconstruction under
realistic through-barrier conditions.

\subsection{Sensor Configuration}
\label{app:mist_sensor}

Data collection took place in a controlled indoor laboratory with approximately
5\,m $\times$ 6\,m usable floor area; subjects performed actions at 2--3\,m
from the door-side sensor array.
Two TI IWR6843 FMCW radars are co-located at door height ($\sim$1.0\,m), one
maintaining a clear line-of-sight for OL0 and one operating through a wooden
barrier of 18\,mm for OL1 or two stacked boards totalling 36\,mm for OL2;
collecting both streams simultaneously ensures that every paired frame is
captured at an identical subject pose.

\subsection{Directory Structure}

Figure~\ref{fig:mist_dir} shows the hierarchical layout of the MIST dataset.
The root contains one sub-directory per subject (S01--S08), and each subject
directory is further divided by action (A01--A30, corresponding to R01--R14 and
D01--D16).
Within each action folder, the paired occlusion design is reflected directly in
the directory structure: one sub-folder pairs OL0 (clear-view) with OL1
(18\,mm board), and a second sub-folder pairs OL0 with OL2 (36\,mm total),
so that every through-obstacle capture has an exact simultaneous clear-view
counterpart at the same pose.
Each occlusion-level folder stores five synchronized modalities: mmWave radar
point clouds (\texttt{.npy}), LiDAR point clouds (\texttt{.bin}), depth maps
(\texttt{.npy}), RGB frames (\texttt{.png}), and a single per-sequence skeleton
annotation file (\texttt{ground\_truth\_h36m.npy}) providing 17-joint H36M
coordinates at 10\,fps.

\subsection{Training Schedule}
\label{app:training}

PRISM is trained in four sequential stages on a single NVIDIA RTX 3090 (24\,GB).
Stage~1 pre-trains SkelKD at a learning rate of $10^{-3}$ with batch size 16 for
5000 epochs (approximately 12\,h) and freezes it.
Stage~2 trains HierVAE independently at $2\!\times\!10^{-4}$ with batch size 8
for 3000 epochs (approximately 48\,h) and freezes it.
Stage~3 jointly optimises the Dynamic Transformer, Condition Network, and GeoAE
with frozen VAE at $2\!\times\!10^{-4}$ with batch size 16 for 5000 epochs
(approximately 24\,h).
Stage~4 fine-tunes all components except SkelKD end-to-end at $3\!\times\!10^{-5}$
with batch size 16 for 2000 epochs (approximately 8\,h).
All stages use Adam with cosine learning-rate decay, gradient clipping at 1.0, and 500 warm-up steps;
Stages~3 and~4 additionally apply EMA with decay 0.9999.

\subsection{Cross-dataset generalization on MM-Fi}
\label{app:mmfi_results}

To evaluate cross-dataset generalisation, all models are trained on MIST-OL0 and
evaluated directly on MM-Fi~\cite{yang2023mmfi}, a large-scale public benchmark
comprising 40 subjects performing 27 action classes across 4 environments with
synchronised mmWave point clouds and depth-camera skeleton ground truth under the
H36M convention.
Evaluation covers the 8 action classes shared between MIST and MM-Fi, namely A03,
A12, A13, A17, A19, A22, A26, and A27, using test subjects S08 to S10 from
environment E01.
Baseline methods are additionally re-trained on MM-Fi subjects S01 to S07 to
provide an in-domain upper bound; PRISM transfers directly from its MIST-trained
weights without any MM-Fi-specific fine-tuning.

Table~\ref{tab:mmfi_appendix} shows that PRISM achieves consistent improvements
on MM-Fi comparable to those on MIST-OL0.
MMD-CD is 40.6\% lower than the best completion baseline PoinTr and COV-CD is
220\% higher, confirming that skeleton-guided conditioning transfers across dataset
conditions without any target-domain fine-tuning.
The 1-NN-CD gap between PRISM and LION narrows compared to MIST, which is expected:
with 7 training subjects in MM-Fi versus 6 in MIST-OL0, unconditional generative
baselines benefit from a richer training distribution; PRISM's skeleton conditioning
still provides the decisive structural advantage.
The SnowflakeNet degradation, with COV-CD 0.081 falling below PCN at 0.086,
replicates the pattern observed on MIST, confirming that multi-stage farthest-point
sampling failure under extreme sparsity is an architectural limitation rather than a
dataset artefact.

\subsection{Through-obstacle robustness on OL1 and OL2}
\label{app:mist_eval}

Table~\ref{tab:mist_per_level} disaggregates the through-obstacle evaluation
by occlusion level, complementing the main-text discussion of OL1/OL2 robustness.
All methods are trained exclusively on MIST-OL0 (P01--P06) and evaluated
zero-shot on OL1 and OL2 with no obstacle-specific adaptation.

At OL1, where a single 18\,mm board attenuates returns to 25--80 points per frame,
PRISM retains a COV-CD of 0.298, a relative drop of 20.5\% from OL0, while all
deterministic baselines drop to COV-CD below 0.07, a relative degradation of
40--50\%.
At OL2, where two stacked 18\,mm boards totalling 36\,mm reduce returns to 10--30 points
per frame, the most severe condition in MIST, PRISM still achieves
a COV-CD of 0.214 while baselines collapse below 0.03.
The $\Delta$COV columns confirm that PRISM's relative degradation is consistently
two to three times smaller than any baseline across both occlusion levels.

Two design choices jointly account for this resilience.
First, GeoAE maps inputs of any density to 128 stable coarse body points,
so geometric anchoring remains intact even when FPS-based aggregation degenerates
under extreme sparsity.
Second, SkelKD's cross-attention aggregation spreads over all visible returns
rather than relying on a nearest-neighbor match, so the skeleton conditioning signal
degrades gracefully as the return count falls rather than producing a catastrophic
feature collapse.
Together, these properties allow PRISM to maintain action-discriminative
reconstruction at point densities where every deterministic baseline produces a
fixed-template output.

Figure~\ref{fig:qual} illustrates the two dominant failure modes of baseline methods.
For distal limb actions such as Wave Hand and Kick, all baselines produce a neutral
standing shape regardless of pose: the sparse radar returns from a raised arm or
swinging leg are insufficient to activate the nearest-neighbour features that
completion backbones rely on, and unconditional generative methods lack the structural
prior needed to distinguish these configurations from rest.
Squat exposes a different failure mode: the full-body postural change distributes
returns across an unusual spatial extent, causing baselines to average over the
observed cloud and yield a blurred, anatomically inconsistent shape.
Per-frame skeleton conditioning steers PRISM toward the correct kinematic state in
all four actions, producing body shapes that faithfully match the LiDAR ground truth
silhouette and limb arrangement.

\subsection{Latent diffusion process}
\label{app:diffusion}

Figure~\ref{fig:diffusion_process} visualises the step-by-step reverse process
in the HierVAE latent space for a single bowing frame.
Panels progress from pure noise at $t\!=\!1.0$ to the fully denoised output at $t\!=\!0$.
Starting from isotropic Gaussian noise, the Dynamic Transformer
progressively resolves global body structure before refining local pose detail.

Across all 30 MIST action classes, actions with large limb displacements,
including Hip Flexion (R07), Forward Kick (D14), and Waving Hand (D11),
establish recognisable global body structure as early as $t\!=\!0.6$,
as skeleton K/V tokens inject a strong kinematic prior at every denoising layer.
Actions with subtle distal motion maintain the correct global silhouette from
$t\!=\!0.6$ but require later timesteps to resolve fine-grained limb configurations.
Per-frame generation remains temporally coherent as SkelKD's joint tokens evolve
smoothly across frames, propagating continuity without explicit temporal smoothing.
